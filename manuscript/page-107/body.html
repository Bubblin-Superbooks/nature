      "More servants wait on man  
 Than he'll take notice of. In every path,  
      He treads down that which doth befriend him  
      When sickness makes him pale and wan.  
 Oh mighty love! Man is one world, and hath  
      Another to attend him."

The perception of this class of truths makes the attraction which draws men to science, but the end is lost sight of in attention to the means. In view of this half-sight of science, we accept the sentence of Plato, that, "poetry comes nearer to vital truth than history." Every surmise and vaticination of the mind is entitled to a certain respect, and we learn to prefer imperfect theories, and sentences, which contain glimpses of truth, to digested systems which have no one valuable suggestion.
